The Sun

Our solar system is made up of the Sun, eight planets, 3 dwraf planets and many asteroid, comets and other space rocks. The Sun is the largest object in the solar system and is located in the centre. The planets, dwarf planets, asteroids and comets travel around the Sun in an ellipse. Our solar system was formed about 5 billion years ago, and the surface of the Sun is about 4.6 billion years old.

Questions

How was the Sun created?

Before it was formed, the Sun and the rest of the solar system was a huge mass of hot gas and dust called a "solar nebula" (a bright area in the night sky that is caused by a mass of dust or gas or by a large cloud of stars that are far away). This nebula spun faster and faster until the clouds of gases, dust and ice particles clumped together and exploded, forming the Sun.

Why does the Sun glow?

The Sun is made up of huge amounts of hydrogen and helium gases. Nuclear reactions at the centre of the Sun emit a large amount of energy that makes the Sun glow. That same energy travels through space and reaches us as heat and light.

How did we learn about the Sun?

We have sent several solar missions in space to study the Sun and its characteristics. The first detailed observations were made by NASA's Pioneer missions that were launched between 1959 and 1968. The Solar Maximum mission of 1980 made a detailed study of "solar flares" (a brief eruption of intense high-energy radiation from the sun's surface, associated with sunspots and causing radio and magnetic disturbances on the earth). The Solar and Heliospheric Obsevatory (SOHO) launched in 1995, has been continuously collecting data regarding the Sun for the last 10 years.


What is a solar flare?

Sometimes the Sun produces a huge amount of magnectic energy that sends outs out jets of gas into space. These jets of gas are class "solar flares" and cause a sudden increase in the brightness of the Sun. Solar flares are often followed by the release of electrically charged particles like protons and electrons. These are called solar winds and are known to travel at a speed of about 500 kilometres per second.

What are sunspots?

Sunspots are storms on the surface of the Sun. These storms appear as huge, dark spots in satelite pictures and so are called sunspots.


CAUTION

Please DO NOT attempt to look at the Sun with your naked eye, sunglasses or other equipments. The ultraviolet (UV) radiations can damage our cornea (outer-most layer of our eye), proteins in the eye lens and retina (light-sensitive cells). An X-Ray sheet can be used to view the Sun as it has the property to abrorb the UV rays coming from the Sun.
